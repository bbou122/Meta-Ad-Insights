📖 What You're Looking At
The Meta Ad Library captures all political and social-issue advertising — not just candidates. That includes oil companies, pharmaceutical giants, Big Tech, and nonprofits all buying the same eyeballs. This section surfaces those hidden power dynamics.
A low cost-per-ad signals a volume strategy — thousands of cheap, A/B-tested creatives targeting broad audiences. A high cost-per-ad signals precision targeting — fewer ads, higher budgets, premium placements.
Some of the most interesting spenders disguise who they are: "Energy Citizens" is the American Petroleum Institute. "America's Plastic Makers" is the American Chemistry Council. "Don't Break What Works" is a Big Tech lobbying group. The disclaimers reveal the real money.
🏭 Industry Lobbying Intelligence
Which industries spend most to shape public opinion on Meta
Why This Matters
Corporate lobbying on social media is often invisible to the public. These aren't product ads — they're narrative ads designed to make you feel a certain way about an industry before legislation, regulation, or elections happen. Oil companies don't run ads saying "drill more" — they run ads about American energy independence and jobs.

⚖️ Cause vs. Corporate — The Narrative Battles

Why This Matters
Every major policy debate has two sides buying ads simultaneously. The ad spend reveals which side is outgunning the other — and by how much. Spending parity doesn't mean equal impact: a well-targeted $5M campaign can outperform a $50M spray-and-pray strategy.

"Astroturfing" — creating fake grassroots organizations — is common in political advertising. The disclaimer field in the Ad Library exposes the real funder. "Energy Citizens" sounds like regular people; the disclaimer reveals it's the American Petroleum Institute. "America's Plastic Makers" is the American Chemistry Council. 📺 Media Ecosystem — Buying Political Audiences
News outlets and media brands spending in political ad space to grow subscribers
Why This Matters
Conservative media outlets — Newsmax, Daily Wire, PragerU — spend heavily in the political ad space not to sell politics directly, but to acquire politically-engaged audiences for their platforms. PragerU alone spent $38.8M. This is audience-building dressed as education. The cost-per-ad metric tells you how aggressively they test creatives: Daily Wire runs high-cost, targeted ads. PragerU runs high-volume, lower-cost content.
